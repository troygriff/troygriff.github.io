Medisync

Tracking and notifying users of harmful drug interactions






Project Overview




Details


Project Type: University of Washington Capstone

Duration: 6 Months

My Role: UX Designer


Background


Medisync was my senior capstone project at University of Washington, which culminated two years of coursework and research in Human Computer Interaction. In Medisync, I served as a UX designer in a team of four people interested in helping those with medical conditions monitor their medication consumption habits.


Details


After six months, our team had developed a fully functioning application that we presented before panel members at the 2015 Informatics Capstone Event. This application allowed those with medical conditions to check for harmful drug interactions and develop proper scheduling habits in a seamless interface.







Project Plan


Before starting the design process, our team solidified a plan over several meetings for how to complete the project as efficiently as possible. This was essential given the allotted time frame for the project and the demands of other coursework leading into the final year of our undergraduate program. Medisync was intended to cover all phases of a product lifecycle and we needed to plan for the design as well as the development of the application. To help with this, I communicated to our project manager what the deliverables and expectations would be from a designer's perspective. We then defined what is in and out of scope by creating user stories, developing a timeline, and ultimately submitting a project proposal. Research




Online Survey


To learn about our potential application user's medication habits and experiences, our team created an anonymous online survey consisting of open-ended and likert-scale questions. The survey received 81 responses from online medical forum users. Respondents to the survey were a wide range of individuals. For example, respondents' ages ranged from 18 to 60 and their medical history covered a variety of health conditions.


Notible Findings

When respondents were asked if they were concerned about drug interactions, 70% said yes, and they typically resorted to calling their doctor or searching online when they were concerned.

One of our respondents researched drug information “To know what I can't mix with, how to take it, what to do with missed dose[s].”

The most common methods of scheduling our respondents used consisted of Google calendar, alarms, and lists on their electronic devices.



The data we gathered affirmed that our application would have immediate utility and positively impact people's lives. A significant amount of the qualitative responses were incorporated into our final designs, improving the overall experience of the intended users.

Subject Matter Expert Interview

Our team met with a Pharmacy Technician at the University of Washington’s hospital to gain perspective from an industry expert on prescription use and scheduling. We asked the Technician a variety of questions to identify problem areas with prescription scheduling and get a better sense of the needs of our potential users. The Technician helped identify potential user consumption habits that we were previously unaware of.


Summary of Findings:

Most of the cases that result in drug interaction are those that are involved with over the counter drugs (OTCs).

According to our interviewee, this is due to the fact that OTC labels often do not document drug interactions well.

There's a need for drug interactions to be listed in a clearer manner and understandable to the general public, such as the interactions between heart medications and OTCs.

Personas


We developed three personas representative of our main user needs, which are: scheduling, reminders, and drug interactions. Demographic information was based on survey results and heart disease statistics. We referred to these three personas when making design decisions and weighing application features.

Research




Initial User Testing


We created paper prototypes to initially analyze how people felt about our application and simulate interactions without having to commit the time to build out a full prototype. This was essential for us, as we were still in the early stages of our design and didn't want to get locked into specific designs without testing it on users first.

I interviewed 10 individuals from our target audiences and had them complete a wide variety of tasks. Notable findings included confusion surrounding labels such “dashboard”, the placement of icons, and home screen content.

Our biggest usability issue was identified when I asked users how they would check to see what medications they had taken that day. Unfortunately, only one user was able to complete this task. With this in mind, we redesigned the homescreen to prominently display the medication schedule. Initially there was a medication tracking chart on the home screen.

Moodboards & Styleguide


Color associations played a large part in the decision making process for visual design. We settled on a mix between inviting colors such as blue and green, while also drawing user attention to key areas with red. Iconography and flat graphics were used frequently throughout the application to convey a modern and cohesive application. The chosen icons helped alleviate the problem with text-heavy traditional medication descriptions.




Visual Comps


These Visual Comps were created by using the selected Moodboard and the Comps closely aligned with our styleguide. This was a key step before handoff to developers for implementation. We also provided annotations specifying colors, sizes, and detailed interactions, to help guide development to be as close to our design vision as possible.






User Testing with Invision


Before the developer handoff, we also conducted additional user tests using the Invision App, which now contained our Comps. Invision better enabled users to understand how our final app would work. This testing allowed us to further iterate and make refinements to the Comps. The refinements reduced the chance of unnecessary development changes, and resulted in more polished final product coming from the design team.

Design Retrospective


Accessibility: Considering our application's user base has a variety of medical conditions, we saw accessibility as an integral component of our design. Our application was designed to accommodate those with motor, cognitive, behavioral, and auditory impairments and enable full usage of the application. We utilized appropriate colors and contrast ratios, large pixel dimensions for buttons, intentionally concise copy, and simplified design features.


Home page design: The challenge for us was prioritizing immediate and current needs like scheduling, but also allow for all main actions to be performed. This was difficult as we wanted to keep things as simple as possible, but early designs for the home screen where very complex. Including a floating action button on the homepage was a key component that helped simplify home page design. From data gathered from user testing sessions, users found this to be the quickest way to add medication. In the case of a new user with no medications scheduled, we found it provides enough affordance for users to start adding to their schedule.


Design Retrospective

User Testing and Design: We conducted a series of user tests throughout the design and development process. This regular user testing provided a clear sense of improvement on the project, which encouraged the team and focused efforts. Regular testing also enabled manageable refinements to the design and reduced the need of drastic last minute changes. Smoother testing experience after the home screen redesign helped us know when to wrap up the design and when the project was ready for development.

Team Coordination: The four person team was a good size for project design. Whiteboarding sessions in particular showed me the value of collaboration on key design features, such as the adding a medication flow.

Management and Implementation: The 6 month project timeframe was longer than I was accustomed to in a classroom or work setting. This enabled a fuller exploration of techniques in user centered design process. We were able to use a variety of user testing techniques, which can be somewhat of a rarity in real projects due to resources/budget concerns.
